# Energy, Work, and Conservation in Physical Systems

This folder supports the Physics knowledge-series article **Energy, Work, and Conservation in Physical Systems**.

The article examines work, kinetic energy, potential energy, conservative and non-conservative forces, system boundaries, power, energy landscapes, equilibrium, SI units, and the broader role of conservation in physical reasoning.

## Repository Purpose

This folder provides advanced research-style computational scaffolding for extending the article's selected examples into reproducible energy-conservation workflows.

## Included Materials

- Python workflows for conservative and damped spring-mass systems, work-energy theorem checks, energy landscapes, and power estimates
- R workflows for spring-mass energy accounting, experimental residual analysis, and measurement-style summaries
- Julia energy-landscape and oscillator scaffolding
- C++ work-energy and spring parameter sweeps
- Fortran spring-energy and power table generation
- SQL metadata for energy systems, constants, process assumptions, source notes, model assumptions, and simulation runs
- Rust command-line utility for work, energy, and power calculations
- C low-level spring-energy example
- reproducible sample datasets
- setup, methodology, and reproducibility documentation

## Core Relations

Work under constant force:

W = F d

General work integral:

W = integral F dot dr

Work-energy theorem:

W_net = Delta K

Kinetic energy:

K = 1/2 m v^2

Spring potential energy:

U = 1/2 k x^2

Near-surface gravitational potential energy:

U = m g h

Mechanical energy:

E = K + U

Conservative force:

F = -dU/dx

Vector conservative force:

F = -grad U

Mechanical-energy balance with non-conservative work:

Delta K + Delta U = W_nc

Average power:

P = W / Delta t

Instantaneous power:

P = F dot v

Damping power:

P_d = -b v^2
